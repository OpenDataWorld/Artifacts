# A Nation Defines Its Neighbours on the Internet

Status: Normative Foundation Principle

## Principle

> On the Internet, a nation defines its neighbours through lawful federation relationships rather than geography alone.

A digital neighbour is any nation, organization, platform, domain, or trusted network with which the nation establishes an explicit, reciprocal, governed relationship through national and federated gates.

## Core Rule

> Geography defines physical neighbours. Trusted federation defines digital neighbours.

## Canonical Model

```text
Nation
  ↓
National Trusted Gate
  ↓
Neighbour Eligibility Check
  ↓
Federation Contract
  ↓
Reciprocal Trusted Gate
  ↓
Digital Neighbour
```

## Digital Neighbourhood

A nation may define digital neighbours based on:

- constitutional and legal compatibility;
- treaty or federation agreements;
- trusted identity and provenance;
- interoperability;
- reciprocal responsibility;
- security and resilience maturity;
- data and privacy protections;
- economic and public-interest value;
- cultural and knowledge exchange;
- reliable exit and dispute resolution.

## Neighbour Eligibility

Before a relationship is admitted, the national gate must check:

1. identity of the prospective neighbour;
2. authority to enter the relationship;
3. jurisdiction;
4. purpose;
5. contract;
6. technical interoperability;
7. safety and conformance;
8. responsibility coverage;
9. reciprocal rights;
10. provenance and evidence;
11. data and infrastructure boundaries;
12. recovery, suspension, and exit.

## Neighbour Is Not Universal Trust

```text
Recognized Neighbour
    ≠
Unlimited Access
```

Every transaction, handoff, agent action, and data exchange still passes an appropriate gate.

Neighbour status creates an eligible relationship framework, not permanent operational authority.

## Types of Digital Neighbours

A nation may recognize:

- treaty neighbours;
- public-service neighbours;
- economic neighbours;
- research and education neighbours;
- infrastructure neighbours;
- security-coordination neighbours;
- cultural neighbours;
- emergency-response neighbours;
- platform and protocol neighbours.

Each relationship has its own purpose, limits, rights, obligations, and expiry.

## Reciprocal Recognition

Digital neighbourhood must be reciprocal.

```text
Nation A recognizes Nation B
+
Nation B accepts Nation A
+
Shared Federation Contract
=
Digital Neighbour Relationship
```

One-sided technical reachability does not create neighbour status.

## National Sovereignty

The nation retains:

- constitutional authority;
- national jurisdiction;
- admission and denial rights;
- data and infrastructure custody;
- the Right to Reason;
- the Right to Deny;
- the right to suspend or restrict;
- the right to recover;
- the lawful right to exit.

## Minimum Necessary Exchange

Neighbour relationships should exchange only what is necessary for the declared purpose.

```text
Digital Neighbourhood
=
Bounded Interoperability
not
Boundary Removal
```

## Agentic Operation

Agents operating across digital neighbours require:

- verified identity;
- domain and jurisdiction mapping;
- platform eligibility;
- ability;
- signed delegation;
- destination acceptance;
- instruction evidence;
- provenance;
- bounded authority;
- expiry and revocation.

Authority does not travel automatically across neighbour boundaries.

## Trusted Gate Maintenance

The national gate maintains neighbour relationships by:

- validating new members;
- renewing contracts;
- monitoring conformance;
- detecting drift;
- restricting degraded relationships;
- revoking unsafe access;
- preserving evidence;
- reconciling shared state;
- closing stale bridges;
- executing lawful exit.

## Neighbour Lifecycle

```text
proposed
→ reviewed
→ recognized
→ active
→ monitored
→ renewed
→ restricted
→ suspended
→ terminated
→ archived
```

No digital-neighbour relationship is permanent by default.

## Dispute and Exit

Every neighbour contract must define:

- dispute resolution;
- applicable jurisdiction;
- independent review;
- emergency restriction;
- data return or deletion;
- continuity obligations;
- settlement;
- migration;
- provenance retention;
- lawful exit.

## Platform Invariants

1. A nation defines digital neighbours through explicit federation contracts.
2. Digital neighbourhood is based on trust, interoperability, and reciprocal responsibility—not geography alone.
3. Neighbour status never grants unrestricted access.
4. Every consequential crossing still passes a trusted gate.
5. Authority remains contextual, bounded, expirable, and revocable.
6. National sovereignty and jurisdiction remain preserved.
7. Only minimum necessary information and capability cross the boundary.
8. Neighbour recognition must be reciprocal.
9. Every relationship has a lifecycle, review, recovery, and exit path.
10. The national gate maintains the neighbourhood continuously.

## Foundation Formula

```text
Digital Neighbour
=
Recognized Identity
+ Accepted Federation Contract
+ Reciprocal Responsibility
+ Trusted Gates
+ Preserved Sovereignty
```

```text
National Digital Neighbourhood
=
Chosen Federated Relationships
+ Bounded Interoperability
+ Continuous Conformance
+ Lawful Exit
```

## Final Statement

> On the Internet, a nation defines its neighbours by choosing the nations, organizations, platforms, and domains with which it will establish lawful, reciprocal, and governed federation relationships. Geography may shape the physical world; trusted gates and explicit contracts shape the digital neighbourhood.
